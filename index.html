What Is a Credit Rating Agency?

3

Credit Rating Agencies (CRAs) are third-party analyzers that assess the creditworthiness of bond issuers and their debt instruments.

Key Functions

  • Provide creditworthiness advisory services
  • Collect and analyze issuer information
  • Publish standardized ratings
  • Reduce information asymmetry between lenders and borrowers

Impact

  • Influence interest rates for borrowers
  • Guide investment decisions
  • Affect market stability and liquidity
  • Integrated into prudential regulations

Credit Rating Agencies serve as critical intermediaries in bond markets

Major Dates in CRA History

4
1909

Moody published first rating manual for railway company bonds using Investor Pay model

1936

Federal bank mandated bonds must be rated investment grade by recognized rating manuals

1960

Moody switched to Issuer Pay Model, changing industry dynamics

1975

SEC created Nationally Recognized Statistical Rating Organization (NRSRO) category

2001

Enron bankruptcy while rated investment grade exposed rating flaws

2006

Congress passed Credit Rating Agency Reform Act in response to SEC inaction

Credit Rating Agencies have evolved through regulatory and market changes

Rating Scale Significance

5

Credit Rating Agencies use standardized letter and number scales to communicate bond risk levels.

For Borrowers

Higher credit ratings lead to lower interest rates, reducing borrowing costs.

For Investors

Lower ratings indicate higher risk, requiring higher potential returns.

For Markets

Small rating changes can significantly impact financial markets due to regulatory reliance.

Rating scales serve as critical communication tools in financial markets

Market Presence of CRAs

6

The credit rating industry is dominated by three major agencies, often referred to as the "Big Three".

US Market (2023)

Total ratings: 2.11 million (down from 2.51 million in 2011)

Big Three share: 94.15% (was 96.42% in 2011)

Nepal Market (2024)

Total ratings: 497 in last financial year

Three agencies: ICRA Nepal, CARE Ratings, Infomerics

"The Big Three's market dominance creates high barriers to entry and limits competition in the credit rating industry."

Market concentration remains extremely high despite regulatory efforts

CRAs as Central Entities

8

Credit Rating Agencies occupy a unique position in financial markets, serving multiple stakeholders.

Trust Mechanism

Historical success in building trust through consistent rating methodologies.

Regulatory Mandate

Government regulations require bond ratings before issuance.

Market Influence

Ratings changes move markets, though this may reflect regulatory reliance rather than pure information value.

"As long as ratings are integrated into prudential regulation, the true value added by major CRAs remains unresolved."

CRAs' centrality stems from both market trust and regulatory requirements

Issuer-Pay Model Concerns

9

The prevailing business model where bond issuers pay for ratings creates potential conflicts of interest.

Conflict of Interest

Issuers may pressure CRAs for favorable ratings to reduce borrowing costs.

Reputation Defense

CRAs maintain rating integrity to protect long-term reputation despite short-term incentives.

Other Limitations

Focus on long-term positions may ignore short-term financial reversals.

The issuer-pay model creates inherent tensions between commercial and analytical priorities

Alternative Business Models

10

Several alternative models have been proposed to address conflicts in the issuer-pay system.

Investor-Pay Model

Subscribers (investors) pay for ratings, aligning incentives with users rather than issuers.

Challenge: Free-rider problem as ratings become public.

Advertising Model

Fee-based service supported by advertising revenue.

Challenge: Potential influence from advertiser interests.

Government Mandated Model

Random assignment of CRAs to issuers by regulatory bodies.

Challenge: Government influence in rating process.

Each alternative model presents trade-offs between independence, funding, and practicality

Competition in CRA Industry

11

The credit rating industry exhibits extremely high concentration with limited competition.

Metric Value Implication
Herfindahl-Hirschman Index 3655 Highly concentrated (10000 = monopoly)
Barriers to Entry Very High Limited by reputation, regulation, and scale
CRARA 2006 Impact Moderate Reduced some barriers but dominance persists
"Increased competition might lead to rating inflation as CRAs compete for issuer business, particularly affecting naive investors."

Market structure challenges complicate efforts to increase competition

CRAs in the Financial Crisis

12

Credit Rating Agencies played a pivotal role in the 2007-2008 financial crisis through their ratings of mortgage-backed securities.

Pre-Crisis (2000-2007)

Assigned AAA ratings to senior MBS tranches based on rising housing market data

Facilitated rapid expansion of private-label MBS market

Crisis (2007-2009)

Massive downgrades as mortgage defaults surged

By mid-2009: 90% of AAA CDOs and 63% of MBS downgraded

"CRAs relied on historical data from a rising market, failing to account for potential housing price declines and correlated defaults."

The crisis exposed critical weaknesses in CRA methodologies and conflicts

Post-Crisis Regulations

13

The financial crisis led to significant regulatory changes affecting Credit Rating Agencies.

2006

CRARA: First major regulation requiring transparency and addressing conflicts

2010

Dodd-Frank Act: Expanded CRA regulation and mandated reduced regulatory reliance on NRSRO ratings

2010-2015

EU Regulations: Similar efforts in Europe to increase oversight and accountability

Regulatory Dilemma

Increased regulation may entrench dominant CRAs by raising compliance costs for new entrants

Key Question

Is regulatory reliance on CRA ratings necessary, or does it create artificial demand?

Regulatory responses have been substantial but their effectiveness remains debated

Conclusions and Recommendations

14

Key Findings

  • CRAs are essential but flawed intermediaries in financial markets
  • Market dominance creates systemic risks and limits innovation
  • Regulatory reliance on ratings may distort market signals
  • Conflicts in issuer-pay model persist despite reforms

Recommendations

  • Gradually reduce regulatory reliance on CRA ratings
  • Encourage alternative business models and competition
  • Enhance transparency in rating methodologies
  • Develop institutional investor capabilities for independent analysis
"Decoupling CRA ratings from prudential regulation could create a more competitive and innovative credit rating industry that better serves financial system needs."

Fundamental reforms are needed to address structural issues in the credit rating industry
